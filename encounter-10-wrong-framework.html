The Wrong Framework

In November 1989, the Berlin Wall fell. It was one of the most consequential events of the twentieth century. It ended the Cold War, reunified Germany, and reshaped the global order.

A structural analysis of the Cold War in October 1989 would have predicted: the Soviet system is under pressure, reform is underway, but the Wall will not fall imminently. The military balance, the institutional inertia, the political incentives — all pointed to continuation.

The structural analysis was wrong. Here is what actually happened.

East Berlin · Internationales Pressezentrum · 9 November 1989 · 18:53

Günter Schabowski, a senior East German Communist Party official, is holding a live press conference. He has just been handed a note. It contains new regulations about travel permissions for East German citizens — a partial, bureaucratic liberalisation, intended to relieve pressure on the regime. The note does not say the Wall is open. It contains conditions, delays, and bureaucratic requirements.

Schabowski has not been briefed on the note. He has just returned from a holiday. He reads it aloud.

An Italian journalist asks: "When does this take effect?"

Schabowski shuffles his papers. He has no information about a timetable. He pauses. Then he says: "Unverzüglich, sofort." Immediately. Without delay.

What do you think happened next?
What happened — minute by minute

19:00 — Schabowski's press conference airs live on West German television, which East Germans can receive. The broadcast is interpreted as: the Wall is open now.

20:00 — Crowds begin gathering at Berlin Wall checkpoints. Guards have received no orders. They call their superiors. Their superiors call their superiors. Nobody knows what to do. The orders say nothing about this.

23:30 — The commander at the Bornholmer Strasse checkpoint, Harald Jäger, faced with thousands of people pressing against his gate, makes a unilateral decision. He cannot hold them and he cannot shoot them. He opens the gate.

00:00 — Other checkpoints follow. East Berliners pour into West Berlin. People begin dismantling the Wall with hammers.

The Berlin Wall fell because a tired bureaucrat, handed a note he hadn't read, gave the wrong answer to a journalist's question. The crowds who heard it on television interpreted it as permission. The guards at the checkpoints, receiving no orders, followed the path of least resistance. Harald Jäger made a decision that no structural analysis could have predicted — because it depended entirely on who Harald Jäger was, at that moment, under that pressure.

The framework this unit uses is built to identify structural forces. What does it miss here?

The structural forces were real. The Soviet system was under genuine pressure. The regime was delegitimised. The economic crisis was severe. All of that is true — and none of it explains why the Wall fell on November 9th rather than November 15th, or March 1990, or not at all.

Structure creates conditions. It does not determine events. Events are made by people making decisions under uncertainty, with incomplete information, in real time. Sometimes those decisions are shaped by structural forces. Sometimes they are shaped by exhaustion, confusion, and a note handed to the wrong person at the wrong moment.

The framework this unit uses is a tool for seeing structural forces. It is a good tool for that purpose. It is not a tool for predicting the specific decisions of specific people on specific nights.

The distinction

Structure explains why something was possible. It does not explain why something happened when it did, in the way it did, because of the people who happened to be there. For that you need history — not just structure, but contingency, personality, accident, and the irreducible specificity of particular moments.

A framework that cannot accommodate accident is not a framework for understanding history. It is a framework for understanding probability. Those are different things.
